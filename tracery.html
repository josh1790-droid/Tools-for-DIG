The growing prevalence of anxiety and depression among young adults highlights an urgent need to examine how digital environments and coping interventions influence mental health outcomes. Recent studies suggest that increased social media exposure is strongly correlated with higher levels of psychological distress, particularly among adolescents and college students who engage in prolonged screen time (Twenge and Campbell 272). Such findings emphasize the importance of identifying protective factors that promote resilience and emotional well-being. Mindfulness-based interventions, which encourage awareness and nonjudgmental attention to one’s thoughts and feelings, have shown considerable promise in reducing stress and improving mood regulation (Kabat-Zinn 148). By integrating mindfulness practices into educational and community settings, this research seeks to explore how self-regulation techniques may buffer the negative mental health effects of excessive digital engagement. Understanding these relationships will contribute to developing accessible, evidence-based strategies that enhance coping skills, promote mental wellness, and foster resilience in populations most affected by the pressures of modern connectivity. Kabat-Zinn, Jon. “Mindfulness-Based Interventions in Context: Past, Present, and Future.” Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice, vol. 10, no. 2, 2003, pp. 144–156. Twenge, Jean M., and W. Keith Campbell. “Associations between Screen Time and Lower Psychological Well-Being among Children and Adolescents: Evidence from a Population-Based Study.” Preventive Medicine Reports, vol. 12, 2018, pp. 271–283.
